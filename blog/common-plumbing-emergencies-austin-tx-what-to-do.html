5 Common Plumbing Emergencies in Austin, TX — And What To Do Before Help Arrives

Plumbing emergencies never happen at a convenient time. Whether it's a burst pipe on a Sunday morning or a backed-up sewer line right before a dinner party, knowing how to respond quickly can mean the difference between a minor inconvenience and thousands of dollars in water damage. 1. Burst Pipes

Austin may be known for its warm climate, but winter freezes — like the devastating 2021 storm — have shown that burst pipes are a very real threat to Texas homeowners.

What to do:
  • Shut off the main water supply immediately. Know where your main shutoff valve is located before an emergency happens. In most Austin homes, it's near the water meter or where the main line enters the house.
  • Turn on all faucets to drain the remaining water in the pipes.
  • Turn off your water heater to prevent damage.
  • Document the damage with photos for your insurance claim.
  • Call a licensed plumber right away.
Burst pipes can release hundreds of gallons of water per hour, so speed is critical. Don't wait — call QuickFix Plumbing at 512-555-0199 the moment you suspect a burst pipe.

2. Sewage Backups

A sewage backup is one of the most unpleasant and potentially hazardous plumbing emergencies a homeowner can face. Signs include multiple drains backing up simultaneously, gurgling sounds from toilets, or sewage odors coming from drains.

What to do:
  • Stop using all water in your home immediately — this includes toilets, sinks, and showers.
  • Avoid contact with backed-up water, as it may contain harmful bacteria.
  • Do NOT use chemical drain cleaners; they can make the problem worse and damage your pipes.
  • Keep children and pets away from affected areas.
  • Call a professional plumber who can use a camera inspection to locate the blockage.
Sewage backups in Austin are often caused by tree root intrusion (a common issue given the area's live oak population), grease buildup, or aging sewer lines. Our team at QuickFix Plumbing has the specialized equipment to diagnose and resolve the issue quickly.

3. Water Heater Failure

Nothing signals a water heater emergency quite like stepping into a cold shower or discovering a puddle of water around your unit. Water heater failures can range from a simple pilot light issue to a full tank rupture.

What to do:
  • If you smell gas near a gas water heater, leave the house immediately and call your gas company.
  • Turn off the cold water supply valve connected to the heater.
  • For electric heaters, switch off the breaker to the unit.
  • Check if the pressure relief valve has been triggered — this can sometimes cause small leaks.
  • Call a plumber to assess whether repair or replacement is needed.
The average water heater lasts 8–12 years. If yours is approaching that age and showing signs of rust or inconsistent heating, it may be time for a proactive replacement. Give us a call at 512-555-0199 and we'll help you explore your options.

4. Overflowing Toilets

An overflowing toilet is stressful, messy, and a potential health hazard. It's usually caused by a clog, a faulty float mechanism, or a deeper sewer line issue.

What to do:
  • Remove the tank lid and push the flapper down to stop water from entering the bowl.
  • Turn off the water supply valve located at the base of the toilet.
  • Use a plunger with a flange (not a cup plunger) to attempt to clear the clog.
  • If multiple toilets are backing up at once, the problem is likely in the main sewer line — call a plumber immediately.
  • Clean and disinfect the area thoroughly after any overflow.
If plunging doesn't resolve the issue within a few attempts, don't keep trying — you could force the clog deeper or damage the toilet. Our Austin plumbing team at QuickFix Plumbing can quickly identify whether the clog is localized or part of a larger sewer issue.

5. Major Leaks Under Sinks or Behind Walls

Leaks under sinks are often discovered when you open a cabinet and find standing water. Hidden leaks behind walls are more insidious — they may go undetected for weeks, causing mold growth and structural damage.

What to do:
  • For under-sink leaks, turn off the shutoff valves beneath the sink and clear out the cabinet.
  • Place buckets or towels to contain the water and minimize damage.
  • Check whether the leak is from the supply lines, drain pipes, or the garbage disposal.
  • For suspected wall leaks, look for bubbling paint, soft drywall, musty odors, or unexplained spikes in your water bill.
  • Do not attempt to open walls yourself — call a professional who can use leak detection equipment.
Pro tip: If your water bill has suddenly increased without a change in your usage habits, there's a good chance you have a hidden leak somewhere in your Austin home.

Be Prepared Before an Emergency Strikes

Here are a few things every Austin homeowner should do before a plumbing emergency occurs:

  • Locate your main water shutoff valve and make sure every adult in the household knows where it is.
  • Inspect your water heater annually for signs of rust, corrosion, or leaks.
  • Avoid flushing anything other than toilet paper down your toilets.
  • Schedule annual plumbing inspections to catch potential problems early.
  • Save your plumber's number in your phone — don't scramble to find one when water is flooding your kitchen.

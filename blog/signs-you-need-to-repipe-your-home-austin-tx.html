7 Warning Signs You Need to Repipe Your Austin, TX Home (And What It Costs)

Most Austin homeowners don't think about their plumbing until something goes wrong. But your pipes — hidden behind walls, under floors, and beneath your yard — have a lifespan. When that lifespan is nearing its end, the warning signs can be subtle at first and then increasingly costly and disruptive.

Repiping a home is one of the most significant plumbing projects a homeowner can undertake, but in many cases, it's far less expensive than dealing with repeated leaks, water damage, and deteriorating water quality. What Is Repiping?

Repiping means replacing some or all of the water supply pipes in your home. It's different from simply fixing a single leak — repiping is typically recommended when the pipes themselves have reached the end of their useful life and are causing systemic, recurring problems.

The most common pipe materials found in older Austin homes include:

  • Galvanized steel — common in homes built before 1970, prone to rust and corrosion
  • Polybutylene (PB) — used in homes built between 1978–1995, known for brittleness and failure
  • Copper — durable but can corrode over time, especially in Austin's hard water conditions
Modern repipes are typically done with PEX (cross-linked polyethylene) or CPVC piping, both of which are durable, flexible, and resistant to corrosion.

7 Warning Signs Your Austin Home May Need Repiping

1. Discolored or Rusty Water

If your tap water runs brown, orange, or yellow — especially when you first turn on the faucet — that's a strong indicator of rust and corrosion inside your pipes. Galvanized steel pipes are particularly prone to this as they age.

While discoloration can sometimes be caused by a water heater issue, persistent rust-colored water from multiple faucets is a clear sign the supply pipes themselves are corroding. This is a health and safety concern — you should not drink or cook with discolored water.

2. Frequent or Recurring Leaks

One leak can be a fluke. Two or three leaks within a year? That's a pattern. If you're calling a plumber repeatedly for different leaks throughout your home, the pipes themselves are failing — not just isolated spots.

At a certain point, patching individual leaks becomes more expensive and disruptive than simply replacing the entire system. Our team at QuickFix Plumbing can help you run the numbers. Call 512-555-0199 to discuss your home's leak history.

3. Consistently Low Water Pressure

Low water pressure throughout your home — not just at one faucet — can indicate that your pipes are significantly corroded or scaled on the inside. Mineral buildup (Austin's water is notoriously hard) and rust deposits gradually narrow the interior diameter of your pipes, restricting flow.

If you've ruled out pressure regulator issues and city supply problems, chronic low pressure is often a repiping indicator.

4. Pipes That Are More Than 50 Years Old

If your Austin home was built before 1970 and has never been repiped, there's a good chance it still has the original galvanized steel plumbing. These pipes have a typical lifespan of 40–70 years — meaning many are overdue for replacement.

Even if you haven't experienced visible symptoms yet, aging pipes are a ticking clock. A proactive repipe can prevent emergency failures and the water damage that comes with them.

5. You Have Polybutylene Pipes

Polybutylene (PB) pipes were widely used in Austin-area homes built between the late 1970s and mid-1990s. If your home is from this era, it may have PB pipes — identifiable by their gray color and flexible texture.

PB pipes are known to degrade and fail unpredictably, often without warning. Multiple class-action lawsuits were filed against PB pipe manufacturers due to widespread failures. If your home has polybutylene plumbing, replacement is strongly recommended regardless of whether you've experienced problems yet.

6. Visible Corrosion on Pipes

If you can see your pipes — in basements, crawl spaces, utility rooms, or under sinks — look for signs of green, white, or blue-green buildup on copper pipes, or orange/brown flaking on galvanized pipes. These are signs of active corrosion.

Visible corrosion on accessible pipes often means worse corrosion is occurring in the sections you can't see. It's worth having a professional take a closer look.

7. Unpleasant Taste or Odor in Your Water

Beyond discoloration, metallic taste or sulfur-like odors in your tap water can indicate pipe degradation. As pipes corrode, they can leach metals like lead, zinc, and iron into your water supply — none of which you want to consume.

If you notice a metallic taste in your water that isn't resolved by your water heater or filtration system, have your pipes inspected immediately.

How Much Does Repiping Cost in Austin, TX?

The cost of repiping depends on several factors, including the size of your home, the type of pipes being installed, the accessibility of your existing plumbing, and whether drywall repair is needed after the work.

General estimates for Austin-area homes:
  • Small home (under 1,500 sq ft): $4,000 – $7,000
  • Medium home (1,500–2,500 sq ft): $7,000 – $12,000
  • Large home (2,500+ sq ft): $12,000 – $20,000+
These are ballpark figures — your actual cost will depend on your specific situation. At QuickFix Plumbing, we provide detailed, transparent quotes with no hidden fees. We'll walk you through exactly what work needs to be done and why.

What to Expect During the Repiping Process

A full repipe typically takes 2–5 days for an average-sized Austin home. Here's what the process generally looks like:

1. Assessment and planning — A plumber maps out your existing plumbing and determines the scope of work. 2. Access points — Small holes are made in walls and ceilings to access the pipes. Modern techniques minimize the number of openings needed. 3. Pipe removal and installation — Old pipes are removed and new PEX or CPVC pipes are run throughout the home. 4. Testing — All new pipes are pressure-tested to ensure there are no leaks before walls are closed. 5. Drywall patching — Access holes are patched and finished. (Some plumbers include this; others coordinate with a drywall contractor.)

During the repipe, your water will be shut off for portions of each workday. Most families remain in their homes throughout the process.

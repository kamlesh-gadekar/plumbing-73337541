How to Lower Your Water Bill in Austin, TX: 10 Plumbing Tips That Actually Work

If you've opened a recent water bill and done a double-take, you're not alone. Austin Water rates have increased steadily over the past several years, and the average Austin household spends significantly more on water than the national average. The good news? Many of the biggest water wasters in your home are plumbing-related — and fixing them can put real money back in your pocket.

At QuickFix Plumbing, we help Austin homeowners identify and eliminate water waste every day. Whether it's a silent toilet leak or an outdated water heater, small fixes can add up to big savings. For a professional plumbing assessment, call us at 512-555-0199.

Here are 10 practical, effective tips to lower your water bill starting today.

1. Fix Leaky Faucets Immediately

A faucet that drips once per second wastes more than 3,000 gallons of water per year — that's like running a full dishwasher over 180 times. Yet many homeowners ignore a dripping faucet because it seems minor.

Leaky faucets are usually caused by worn washers, O-rings, or cartridges — all inexpensive parts that a plumber can replace quickly. Don't ignore the drip. Over the course of a year, it's costing you more than you realize.

2. Check for Silent Toilet Leaks

Toilets account for nearly 30% of indoor water use, and a running toilet can waste up to 200 gallons per day without you even noticing. These leaks are called "silent" because the water flows directly from the tank into the bowl without any obvious dripping sound.

How to test for a silent toilet leak:
  • Add a few drops of food coloring to your toilet tank.
  • Wait 15–20 minutes without flushing.
  • If color appears in the bowl, you have a leak.
The culprit is usually a worn flapper valve — a simple, inexpensive fix. If you have multiple toilets in your home, test all of them. Call QuickFix Plumbing at 512-555-0199 to get them all inspected and repaired in one visit.

3. Upgrade to WaterSense-Certified Fixtures

Older showerheads can use up to 2.5 gallons per minute (GPM), while WaterSense-certified models use 1.8 GPM or less — with no noticeable difference in pressure or comfort. The same applies to faucets and toilets.

Upgrading just your showerhead can save a family of four up to 2,900 gallons per year. When combined with WaterSense faucet aerators and a high-efficiency toilet, the savings become substantial.

Austin Water also offers rebates for qualifying water-efficient fixture upgrades — so you save on installation and your monthly bill.

4. Insulate Your Hot Water Pipes

When you turn on your hot water faucet, you often run the water for 30–60 seconds waiting for it to heat up. All that cold water going down the drain is wasted. Insulating your hot water pipes reduces heat loss so hot water arrives at your faucets faster.

Pipe insulation is a low-cost upgrade that also reduces energy consumption from your water heater — a double win for your utility bills.

5. Consider a Tankless Water Heater

Traditional tank water heaters keep 40–80 gallons of water hot around the clock, even when you're at work or asleep. Tankless (on-demand) water heaters heat water only when you need it, reducing energy use by 24–34% for most households.

While the upfront cost is higher than a traditional unit, the long-term savings on both your water heating and energy bills make it a smart investment for many Austin homeowners. Ask our team at QuickFix Plumbing whether a tankless upgrade makes sense for your home — just call 512-555-0199.

6. Install a Whole-Home Water Pressure Regulator

High water pressure feels great in the shower, but it forces more water through your pipes and fixtures than necessary — and puts added stress on your plumbing system, increasing the risk of leaks.

The ideal home water pressure is between 40–60 PSI. If your pressure is running higher (you can check with an inexpensive gauge from a hardware store), a pressure reducing valve (PRV) can be installed to bring it into range. This reduces water consumption AND extends the life of your appliances and fixtures.

7. Run Only Full Loads of Dishes and Laundry

While this is a behavioral tip, your plumbing setup matters too. Make sure your washing machine supply hoses are in good condition and that connections are tight — a slow leak behind your washer can go undetected for months.

Also consider upgrading to an ENERGY STAR-certified washing machine, which uses significantly less water per load than older models.

8. Water Your Lawn Smarter

In Austin's hot climate, outdoor irrigation can account for up to 50% of household water use during summer months. A few plumbing upgrades can help:

  • Install a smart irrigation controller that adjusts watering schedules based on weather data.
  • Add drip irrigation to garden beds and trees for highly efficient watering.
  • Check your irrigation system for broken sprinkler heads and leaks each spring — even a single broken head can waste thousands of gallons.
QuickFix Plumbing can inspect and repair your irrigation system connections and outdoor faucets to ensure nothing is leaking.

9. Check Your Water Meter for Hidden Leaks

If your water bill is high but you can't identify the source, check your water meter directly. Here's how:

  • Turn off all water in your home.
  • Check your water meter and note the reading.
  • Wait 1–2 hours without using any water.
  • Check the meter again — if it has moved, you have a leak somewhere.
This is one of the fastest ways to confirm whether a hidden leak exists. If your meter moves, call a plumber to locate and fix it before more water — and money — goes to waste.

10. Schedule an Annual Plumbing Inspection

The single most effective way to keep your water bill low and your plumbing system healthy is a professional annual plumbing inspection. A licensed plumber can:

  • Detect slow leaks and drips you may have missed
  • Assess the condition of supply lines, shutoff valves, and drain pipes
  • Check water pressure and identify inefficiencies
  • Recommend upgrades that offer the best ROI for your specific home
